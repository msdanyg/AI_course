Here's a scene you are probably familiar with.

You paste a giant email thread into Claude. Type "what should I do?" And wait.

The response comes back, and it's completely useless. It somehow fixated on a throwaway comment instead of the actual business problem.

<break time="0.5s" />

Sound familiar?

This isn't the AI being dumb. This is a predictable result of a mental model mismatch.

Today, you'll learn why AI works the way it does, and how to work WITH that reality instead of against it.

<break time="2s" />

Most of us grew up with Google: Type keywords. Get links. Click the best match.

This mental model is so ingrained that we bring it to AI without thinking.

But ChatGPT isn't Google. It doesn't retrieve answers from a database. It generates responses by predicting the next word in the sentence based on everything you've given it.

Think of it like this: when you type "ActivTrak competitors," Google finds pages containing that text. 

Claude predicts what words would most likely follow that phrase based on patterns it learned during training. A training which, by the way, may have taken place some time ago.

<break time="1s" />

A search engine retrieves existing content. More keywords means better results.

A reasoning engine generates new content. More words can actually cause confusion.

<break time="1s" />

Here's why this matters.

When you dump everything into a prompt, you're not giving the AI "more to work with." You're giving it more possible paths to wander down, most of them wrong.

One technical term to describe how AI work is "probabilistic reasoning engine." Your job is to constrain those probabilities so the high-probability paths lead where you actually want to go.

This is why prompt engineering matters so much. Prompt engineering is the practice of carefully crafting a prompt to deliver a better response.

<break time="2s" />

Here is a classic example that illustrates this point.

Three glasses. Each contains water and an object: a paperclip, scissors, a pocket watch. All appear to have the same water level. Which glass actually contains the most water?

<break time="2s" />

You probably got it instantly. The watch displaces more water, so that glass looks full but actually contains less. The paperclip glass has the most water.

With the recent addition of reasoning models, our generative AI tools can now solve this. But they can still get it wrong if you add more details, like who's drinking the water, the weight of the scissors, and so on.

AI models can fail because irrelevant details in the prompt scatter the model's attention away from the core physics problem.

<break time="1s" />

Remember, your AI assistant tries to predict the correct answer by analyzing the words you give it and guessing which words are most likely to be the right answer. It does not create a mental model of the setting or imagine what might be taking place. So the words you choose matter as much as the topic you are presenting it with.

In your daily work, this problem surfaces when you post a twenty-message email thread and ask for a "strategic recommendation."

Claude's attention scatters across holiday party logistics, personnel gossip, emotional subtext, AND the actual business question.

The response addresses all of these poorly instead of any of them well.

<break time="1s" />

The fix? EXPLICIT focus.

Tell Claude what to focus on. Tell it what to ignore. Concentrate its attention on the reasoning that matters.

For example: "Identify the three highest-priority roadmap items. Ignore discussion of social events or personnel changes."

<break time="2s" />

The Water Glass Effect reveals why the Solo Pilot approach fails for complex work.

Solo Pilots treat every AI interaction as an isolated event. Paste everything. Hope for the best. 

<break time="0.5s" />

This creates three problems.

One: Context bleeds everywhere. Instructions mix with data. Background contaminates the task.

Two: Errors cascade unchecked. Misunderstand step one? Everything after builds on that mistake.

Three: No specialization. One "generalist" AI tries to research, strategize, write, AND critique simultaneously. It does all of them poorly.

<break time="1.5s" />

Squadron Leaders orchestrate AI as a system.

Break complex tasks into specialized stages. Feed structured context appropriate to each stage. Verify before proceeding.

Each agent excels at its function. The Squadron Leader coordinates their outputs into something none could produce alone.

Solo Pilots use one prompt and hope for the best. Results vary wildly. It's individual skill dependent.

Squadron Leaders use coordinated workflows. They achieve consistent quality through process-encoded expertise.

<break time="2s" />

Every AI conversation happens within a "context window," the total information the model can hold at once. Claude's is enormous. About five hundred pages worth.

The trap is thinking more context equals better results.

Research shows that information PLACEMENT matters enormously. Beginning and end get high attention. The middle? It gets lost.

A bad prompt looks like this: everything dumped together, no structure, no priorities.

A good prompt looks like a mission briefing.

Opening orientation: Who you are, what we're doing.

Core data: The specific information needed.

Clear instructions: What to do with that data.

Output specs: Format and constraints.

<break time="1s" />

Same information. RADICALLY different results.

<break time="2s" />

These principles map directly to our core value: Insights, Not Oversight.

Consider the difference between these two framings.

Oversight framing: "Analyze this data to find who's underperforming."

Insight framing: "Identify teams that might need additional support or resources."

<break time="1.5s" />

The AI follows your lead. Frame the task as surveillance? You'll get surveillance-style outputs. Frame it as support? You get consultative insights.

When you orchestrate your Squadron, using Gemini for grounded research, Claude for strategic reasoning, Granola for meeting context, You get to the information you need presented in the form you need it. 

Structure enables insight. Chaos enables noise.

<break time="2s" />

Three things to remember.

One: AI is a reasoning engine, not a search engine. Structure your context. Don't just dump keywords.

Two: The Water Glass Effect is real. Irrelevant context degrades AI reasoning. Curate ruthlessly.

Three: Solo Pilots improvise. Squadron Leaders orchestrate. Complex work requires specialized agents and structured handoffs.
