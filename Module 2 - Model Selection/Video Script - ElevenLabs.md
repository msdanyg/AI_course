# Module 2: Model Selection & The Thinking Protocol
## ElevenLabs Narration Script

**Duration:** ~6:00
**Recommended Model:** Eleven Flash v2 or Eleven Turbo v2
**Speed:** 1.0 (default)

---

Sarah needs to check if a company is in our ICP. She opens Claude, selects Opus with Extended Thinking, and waits thirty seconds for a question Haiku could have handled in two.

Meanwhile, Marcus is debugging a complex pricing model using Haiku because "it's faster." Three hours later, he's still getting wrong answers.

One overspent by one hundred times. The other wasted three hours. Neither was wrong for using Claude. They were wrong for using the WRONG Claude.

<break time="1.5s" />

Claude isn't one model. It's a fleet.

Haiku is your Scout. Fast, cheap, perfect for quick lookups and bulk categorization. Costs roughly one sixtieth of Opus.

Sonnet is your Daily Driver. Code generation, analysis, everyday writing. This is where you'll spend most of your time.

Opus is your Strategist. Deep reasoning, complex multi-step analysis. Deploy when the path to solution is unclear.

<break time="1.5s" />

Beyond model selection, there's another lever: HOW the model thinks.

Standard mode is fast pattern-matching. Usually sufficient.

Extended Thinking is deliberate reasoning. Claude gets a scratchpad, works through the problem step-by-step before committing to an answer. Takes longer, costs more, but catches errors that standard mode misses.

<break time="1.5s" />

How do you choose? Two questions.

First: Task complexity. Simple lookup, standard work, or strategic analysis?

Second: Error tolerance. Will you review this, or does it go straight to stakeholders?

"Translate this email." Simple, you'll review. Haiku.

"Build a dashboard." Standard work, iteration needed. Sonnet.

"Analyze our churn increase." Complex, going to executives. Opus with Extended Thinking.

Match resources to requirements. Don't default to the fanciest option.

<break time="1.5s" />

When you use Extended Thinking, Claude's reasoning becomes visible in a collapsible block.

Good thinking: Breaking problems into steps. Checking intermediate results. Self-correction.

Warning signs: Rushing to conclusion. Confident assertions without verification.

For high-stakes tasks, always expand the thinking block before accepting the output.

<break time="1.5s" />

Three mistakes to avoid.

Opus for everything. You're overpaying for simple tasks.

Haiku for complex reasoning. You'll waste hours getting wrong answers.

Skipping the thinking block. Five seconds of scanning catches errors you'd otherwise miss.

<break time="1s" />

Three things to remember.

Claude is a fleet. Match the aircraft to the mission.

Extended Thinking buys accuracy with time. Use it for math and complex logic.

Read the thinking block. It's your verification layer.

Your fleet is briefed. See you in the lesson.
