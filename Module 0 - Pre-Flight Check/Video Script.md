## SECTION 2: THE TRAFFIC LIGHT PROTOCOL (3 minutes)

**[SCREEN: "Data Safety" header with traffic light graphic]**

**INSTRUCTOR (V.O.):**
"Now for the most important concept in this module: what you can share with AI tools.

**[SCREEN: "Input vs. Output" diagram]**

Here's the key insight: The risk is rarely in analyzing data. The risk is in sharing unreviewed AI output.

Think about it—if you upload customer data to Claude for analysis, that data stays in your conversation. But if you copy the AI's response directly into an email without reviewing it, that's where problems can occur.

**[SCREEN: Quote - "AI drafts. Humans send."]**

This leads to our core principle: AI drafts, humans send. You clear missions for takeoff. You own what leaves the runway.

**[SCREEN: Traffic light showing three zones]**

Let's walk through the three zones.

**[SCREEN: Green light highlighted]**

Green Zone: Go freely. This includes customer names, emails, deal information. Internal documents like meeting notes and project plans. Usage data and analytics. Business communications.

Why is it green? This information helps you do your job. The AI processes it privately. As long as you review outputs before sharing externally, using this data is not only safe—it's expected.

**[SCREEN: Yellow light highlighted]**

Yellow Zone: Review required. This includes marketing collateral with customer PII, competitive intelligence with source details, and third-party data under NDA.

Why yellow? You can analyze it, but outputs might need PR, Legal, or partner review before external distribution.

**[SCREEN: Red light highlighted]**

Red Zone: Never share. API keys, passwords, admin credentials, security configurations. If in doubt, don't include it.

**[SCREEN: Decision flowchart]**

When you're about to paste something into AI, ask: What is this data? What zone is it in? What will I do with the output? Have I reviewed before sending?

If it's green and you're reviewing: proceed confidently."

**[SCREEN: "Inventory Your Tools" header]**

**INSTRUCTOR (V.O.):**
"Your cockpit will look different from your colleagues'—that's expected. Let's identify what you have.

**[SCREEN: Tool inventory checklist by role]**

First, inventory your tools by Squadron role. For Mission Control—reasoning and writing—do you have Claude, ChatGPT, Claude Code, or something else? For Recon and Radar—research—do you have Gemini, Bing AI? For Flight Recorder—meeting capture—Granola, Chorus, Zoom AI?

**[SCREEN: Configuration steps]**

For each tool you have, verify it's working. Log in, run a test prompt, check if you can upload files, note any special features.

**[SCREEN: Gap analysis table]**

Not everyone has tools in every role. If you're missing Mission Control, request Claude or ChatGPT from IT. Gemini is available company-wide for research. Zoom AI summaries work for everyone.

**[SCREEN: Restricted tools warning]**

One important note: some AI tools are not approved for ActivTrak use. DeepSeek AI and RedNote AI apps have security concerns. When in doubt about a tool, check with IT.

**[SCREEN: Verification checklist]**

Before moving on, confirm you know which tools you have for each role, they're configured and working, and you understand the traffic light zones. Module 1 assumes your cockpit is ready."

## SECTION 4: THE SQUADRON MINDSET (2 minutes)

**[SCREEN: "Solo Pilot vs. Squadron Leader" split screen]**

**INSTRUCTOR (V.O.):**
"With tools configured and data boundaries understood, let's establish the mindset that distinguishes Solo Pilots from Squadron Leaders.

**[SCREEN: Solo Pilot side - simple questions]**

The Solo Pilot approaches AI as a question-answer machine: 'Write me an email.' 'Summarize this.' 'What should I say?' They get generic outputs, restart conversations constantly, and never build reusable workflows.

**[SCREEN: Squadron Leader side - structured requests]**

The Squadron Leader orchestrates AI as a reasoning system: 'Given this account's history and our expansion goals, draft a QBR email that addresses...' They provide context, structure requests, and review everything.

**[SCREEN: Comparison table]**

The differences matter. Solo Pilots provide minimal context; Squadron Leaders provide rich background. Solo Pilots use freeform questions; Squadron Leaders use organized structures. Solo Pilots start fresh every time; Squadron Leaders maintain persistent knowledge.

**[SCREEN: "The rest of this course teaches you to operate as a Squadron Leader"]**

The rest of this course teaches you to operate as a Squadron Leader. But the mindset starts now."
